Q Routing is typically department/ministry-level — no hyperlocal routing to ward engineer, contractor, or specific crew.
Problem
Right now, routing of complaints usually stops at the department or ministry level (e.g., sanitation dept., public works).
●	This means the issue is not directly assigned to the ward-level engineer, local contractor, or field crew actually responsible.

●	It causes delays because staff at higher levels must manually forward the complaint.

●	Citizens experience slower resolutions and lack clarity on who is handling the issue.

________________________________________
Better Solution
●	Implement hyperlocal routing logic:

○	Use ward boundaries, GPS/geotagging, and local jurisdiction data to map complaints to the exact responsible officer/crew.

○	Example: pothole in Ward 15 goes directly to the ward engineer or contractor, not just to the central Public Works Department.

●	Maintain an administrative hierarchy so supervisors can still monitor escalations if local staff don’t respond.

●	Allow auto-escalation: if the ward-level engineer doesn’t act in X hours, the complaint bubbles up to higher authorities.

●	Higher operational risk during onboarding of new hires or rollout of major updates.

Recommendation:
●	Provide a dedicated sandbox/staging environment with anonymized or synthetic data.

●	Implement role-based access where training accounts cannot affect production systems.

●	Add guided tutorials and simulations inside the sandbox (gamified learning, walkthroughs).

●	Ensure regular synchronization with new features so staff can practice upcoming changes before they go live.

●	Track training KPIs (time to proficiency, error rate reduction) to measure impact.
